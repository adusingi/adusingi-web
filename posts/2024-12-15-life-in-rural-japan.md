# Code, Craft & Culture: Building from Rural Japan

Most developers dream of working from San Francisco, New York, or Tokyo. I chose rural Okayama, Japan—and it's one of the best decisions I've made.

## Why Rural Japan?

After years working in Kyoto and Tokyo, I moved to Okayama Prefecture in 2019. Here's what drew me:

- **Lower cost of living**: More space, less stress
- **Rich culture**: Traditional crafts, festivals, local artisans
- **Natural beauty**: Mountains, coastline (Ushimado), countryside
- **Calm pace**: Time to think, create, and practice Tai-Chi (太極拳)

## The Remote Work Reality

Working remotely from rural Japan has unique challenges and advantages:

### Challenges

- **Time zones**: Client calls at odd hours (SF = 17 hours behind)
- **Language barriers**: Not all services speak English
- **Limited tech events**: No meetups or conferences nearby
- **Slow internet** (sometimes): Rural areas can have connectivity issues

### Advantages

- **Deep focus**: Fewer distractions, more productive hours
- **Lower expenses**: More runway for experiments and side projects
- **Cultural immersion**: Daily interaction with Japanese culture
- **Quality of life**: Fresh air, nature, slower rhythm

## What I'm Building

Living here has influenced what I build:

### [Bento Kumiko](https://www.bentokumiko.com)
A curated guide to restaurants, artisans, and stays across Japan. Born from my own explorations and recommendations I gave to visiting friends.

### [Ushimado Town](https://www.ushimadotown.com)
A directory for my local area—highlighting businesses and events in this small coastal town. Helping the community get online.

### Travel Planning Services
Organizing team retreats and custom Japan experiences for remote teams who want to work from somewhere inspiring.

## The Tai-Chi Connection (太極拳)

Practicing Tai-Chi with Master Kiyoko Kashiwagi has taught me patience, discipline, and focus—qualities that translate directly to software development.

The slow, deliberate movements mirror the process of writing clean code. The emphasis on balance reflects the need to balance work with life.

## For Other Remote Builders

If you're considering working from rural Japan:

1. **Visit first**: Spend a month to see if the lifestyle fits
2. **Learn basic Japanese**: Google Translate only gets you so far
3. **Build community**: Join local clubs, volunteer, connect
4. **Embrace the pace**: Don't fight the slower rhythm—use it
5. **Stay connected**: Regular video calls with collaborators

## Conclusion

You don't need to be in a tech hub to build great products. Sometimes the best work happens away from the noise, in places that give you space to think and create.

Rural Japan offers that space—plus incredible culture, food, and natural beauty.
